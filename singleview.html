Wild Apple

Wild apple is a collective term for all the mysterious, almost inedible and really delicious apples that grow sporadically around the Danish forests and grasslands. They are nameless apples that nature itself has produced.

How to recognize wild apples

  1. The small white or pink flowers.
  2. The small apples are rarely more than 3 to 4 cm in diameter.
  3. Their shape is either a bush or a small tree.

Where?

Deciduous forests, coniferous forests, cities, fences.

When?

September & October

In nature

Wild apples grow in brown soil near forests, parks, and gardens. They come from cultivated apples that are mixed with other apples.

They can be shrubs or small trees with small, white or pink flowers. Most are small, around three to four cm in diameter. If the apple doesn't come off easily when you pull it, it's not ripe. A perfectly picked apple has only a short stem, without leaves or twigs..

Sensory

Wild apples come in different flavors - some are sour, some sweet with hints of honey and flowers, and some even taste bitter. Explore and discover the ones that match your preference or enjoy the surprises.

They vary in texture, from crunchy and crisp to soft and floury. Their skin ranges from tough and thick to paper-thin. Some have many seeds in sturdy cores, while others are almost all pulp.

In the kitchen

Wild apples vary in how they respond to cooking, so it's fun to experiment. Generally, apples handle heat well.

When fried, their sweetness shines, and the surface almost glazes at high heat. The skin and core hold many of the exciting flavors, so try including them. Boil them with some liquid and collect the flavorful water.

Apples work in sweet and savory dishes, like apple pork, apple cake, or salads, adding a unique edge and freshness.
